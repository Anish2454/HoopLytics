  • The Hack-A-Shaq Strategy

    Today I’d like to talk about something that many viewers of the NBA question:

    The Hack-A-Shaq strategy.

    First off, what is it? The Hack-A-Shaq strategy involves fouling the opposing team’s worst free throw shooter on purpose so that the team loses a chance to score their points in a regular offensive setting and is instead forced to rely on free throws.

    Many might argue that the Hack-A-Shaq strategy is ineffective because most bad free throw shooters hover around the 50% mark, which means that they will on average get one point per trip to the line. So, why even give them the chance to score that one point? Wouldn’t it be much better to play good defense and prevent any scoring at all? Let’s take a look at the numbers to see how effective the strategy really is.

    PPP, or points per possession, is a common statistic used in the NBA to determine the offensive rating of a team. The higher a team’s PPP, the more capable they are of scoring the basketball, and the better they are on offense. Conversely, the amount of points a team gives up is known as defensive rating. The lower a team’s defensive rating, the less points they give up, and the better the defense.

    Using these statistics, we can start to pick apart how good the Hack-A-Shaq strategy really is. Let’s start with a common example: Clippers Vs. Spurs. The San Antonio Spurs are known to use the Hack-A-Shaq strategy religiously and the Clippers, along with Deandre Jordan, are notorious for being victims of the strategy. Here are the numbers:

    1) The Clippers had an offensive rating of 1.12 last year, meaning they scored 1.12 PPP.

    2) When the spurs send Deandre Jordan to the free throw line, he gets 2 shots each with a 48.2% chance of going in.

    3) Shooting 48.2% at the line is equivalent to a “Points Per Free Throw” of .482.

    4) Since Deandre Jordan gets two attempts, .482 * 2 = .964 PPP

    5) That .964 PPP is significantly lower than the Clipper’s usual 1.12 PPP.

    6) In fact, Deandre Jordan would have to shoot around 61% from the line in order for the strategy to fail.

    There you have it. Until the NBA changes the rules to ban the Hack-A-Shaq strategy, or players start hitting their free throws, you can expect to see the strategy used all the time.
